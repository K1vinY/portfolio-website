Applied Statistical Testing on Socioeconomic Impact Using NHANES Data

Aug 2024 - Dec 2024
Arizona State University
Data Analysis

Abstract

This project investigates how income level affects various health and lifestyle outcomes by analyzing over 10,000 records from the NHANES dataset. Using statistical techniques including ANOVA and Chi-square tests, the study revealed significant disparities across income groups in sleep duration, blood pressure, substance use, and depression. The findings support the hypothesis that higher-income individuals tend to experience better physical and emotional health, highlighting the crucial role of socioeconomic factors in public health.

The What

Do wealthier individuals actually live healthier, less stressful lives? Motivated by both personal observation and public health research, this study explores whether income is significantly associated with measurable health and lifestyle outcomes. Key variables include sleep duration, blood pressure, regular marijuana use, and depression levelsâ€”each of which serves as an indicator of stress or emotional well-being.

The How

Using the NHANES dataset, four health metrics were selected and cleaned using R. ANOVA was applied to continuous variables (e.g., sleep hours and systolic blood pressure), while Chi-square tests examined categorical outcomes (e.g., marijuana use and depression frequency). The analysis included 12 income groups and more than 10,000 participants. Data were visualized using ggplot2 with histograms, boxplots, and bar charts to uncover patterns and distributional insights.

Results & Impact

The statistical tests revealed strong associations between income and all selected health indicators:

  • Sleep Duration: Significantly longer in higher-income groups (p < 0.001)
  • Blood Pressure: Statistically lower in several higher-income groups
  • Marijuana Use: Less frequent among wealthy individuals (p < 0.001)
  • Depression Levels: Substantially lower among top income tiers (p < 2.2e-16)

These results confirm the hypothesis that wealth may offer protective benefits for both physical and emotional health. The project underscores the importance of socioeconomic equity in healthcare policy and supports using data science for population health research.
